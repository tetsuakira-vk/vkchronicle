Mejibray's aggressive fusion of djent-influenced metal riffing with visual kei's theatrical presentation created something genuinely confrontational—a band that sounded as visually jarring as they looked. Founded in Tokyo by vocalist Tsuzuku (formerly of VanessA) in 2011, Mejibray crystallized from a solo project into an official four-piece band on June 18, 2011, immediately establishing themselves as contenders in the increasingly metal-leaning wing of visual kei. The roster featured MiA on guitar and Meto on drums, though guitarist Ippu's departure just months later set the tone for the instability that would eventually define their trajectory.

Mejibray's recorded output, though modest in volume, documented a band refining its approach to sonic heaviness. Their 2012 debut Emotional【KARMA】 established their foundation—dense, distortion-laden compositions that bridged djent precision with visual kei's melodic sensibilities. By 2014, with THE "420" THEATRICAL ROSES and the concise SM, the band had tightened their attack, embracing what fans termed "loud kei" and "menhera kei," subgenres emphasizing raw emotional intensity and psychological vulnerability delivered through overwhelming instrumentation. The 2017 follow-up SM #2 represented their final statement before everything fractured, showcasing a band still hungry to push their metallic palette further.

What made Mejibray significant within the broader visual kei landscape was their refusal to soften metal's edges for visual accessibility. They inhabited the same underground Tokyo circuit as genre stalwarts like DADAROMA, yet their commitment to djent's mathematical aggression felt fresher, almost imported from Western metalcore while remaining unmistakably Japanese in their visual presentation and thematic preoccupations. They represented a faction of visual kei artists who viewed the aesthetic not as window dressing but as an equal partner to sonic brutality.

The band's December 2017 hiatus—initiated when Tsuzuku and bassist Koichi terminated their label contracts—ended what many fans hoped would be a temporary pause. Years have passed since, and while reunion announcements never materialized, Mejibray's discography remains essential listening for anyone exploring visual kei's heavier territories. Their influence persists among younger bands navigating the djent-metal intersection, and devoted fans still debate whether the pause might yet become something more. For now, Mejibray endures as a time capsule of a specific moment when visual kei's experimental impulses reached their most sonically uncompromising.
