Jiluka wields the distorted guitar and electronic pulses of modern metal like a surgical instrument, carving out a niche that refuses easy categorization. Born from a Tokyo session band in May 2013, they emerged as something the scene hadn't quite heard before: metalcore aggression fused with the theatrical sensibilities of visual kei and the synthetic textures of digital rock. Their journey from debut to their current incarnation reveals a band willing to evolve rather than ossify within genre constraints.

The core lineup solidified in 2015 when drummer Zyean joined vocalist Ricko (formerly known as Kanata), guitarist and rapper Sena, and bassist Boogie. That restart marked a turning point; their early work under the "Modern Metal Visual Kei" banner gave way to a more precise identity: Electro Gothic Metal, a term that finally captured their hybrid DNA. The albums that followed—*Xenomorphic* (2017), *Metamorphose* (2018), and *XANADU* (2019)—showcased a band growing confident in their sonic language, layering djent-influenced riffing with electronic atmospherics and Ricko's visceral vocal delivery.

What distinguishes Jiluka within the broader visual kei landscape is their refusal to default to traditional aesthetics or narrative. They're neither retro-romantics nor trend-chasers; instead, they've carved a path that honors metal's heaviness while embracing the genre-fluid experimentation Japanese rock has always championed. Their work alongside their own independent label, Faizh Music, speaks to artistic autonomy—a choice that grants them freedom but also demands they maintain listener engagement without major label machinery.

With eight albums now in their catalog and a recent 2025 release in *XNDII*, Jiluka remains actively engaged in their creative project. The band's longevity in an era of constant lineup shuffles and genre fragmentation matters precisely because they've stayed true to evolution rather than repetition. For Western fans discovering visual kei beyond the scene's most documented acts, Jiluka represents the genre's ongoing capacity for invention—proof that you don't need historical weight or mainstream recognition to make vital, forward-thinking music. They're a band that asks what metal can sound like when you remove its orthodoxies and let it breathe alongside synth, rap, and gothic atmosphere.
