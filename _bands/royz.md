Royz crashes headlong into visual kei's sonic landscape with the kind of aggressive digital rock energy that refuses easy categorization—they're simultaneously harsh and hypnotic, wielding distortion and synthesizers like weapons designed to destabilize rather than comfort. Formed in 2009 in Osaka, the band has remained committed to this abrasive aesthetic across their entire career, building a devoted following among fans who crave intensity over accessibility. The current lineup features vocalist Subaru commanding the microphone with theatrical conviction, guitarist Kuina laying down searing riffs, bassist Koudai anchoring the low end, and drummer Tomoya driving the percussion with mechanical precision, all operating under the B.P Records banner.

The band's early albums established their identity within the louder end of visual kei. *Revolution to New AGE* (2011) announced their arrival as uncompromising sonic architects, while *Tears* (2013) and *CORE* (2014) refined their assault on conventional song structures, blending elements of hardcore punk with visual kei's theatrical presentation. By the mid-2010s, releases like *S.I.V.A* (2016) and *RAVEN* (2017) demonstrated their willingness to experiment with digital production techniques and electronic textures without sacrificing the raw energy that defines them. *WORLD IS MINE* (2018) solidified their reputation as consistent innovators—a band willing to push their own boundaries while maintaining the visceral impact their audience expects.

Within the broader visual kei ecosystem, Royz occupies crucial territory. They're part of a lineage that includes acts like Dir en Grey and Merzbow's intersections with rock, yet they've carved their own path by embracing what some might dismiss as "too loud" or "too inaccessible." In an era when visual kei occasionally risks dilution into aesthetics-first pop-rock, their commitment to actually difficult, challenging music has earned them cultural significance as keepers of the genre's harder traditions. Their sound matters because it refuses compromise in an industry that often demands it.

Still active and releasing material—most recently *Lync* in 2022—Royz continues to prove that aggressive, unpolished artistry has enduring value. They remain essential listening for anyone serious about understanding visual kei's full spectrum, a band that understands the genre's capacity for genuine catharsis through controlled chaos.
