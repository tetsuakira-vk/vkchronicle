Dolly emerged as a distinctive presence in the Japanese visual kei scene, establishing themselves through a unique fusion of rock instrumentation with French linguistic and cultural influences. The band's incorporation of French language vocals and aesthetic sensibilities set them apart from their contemporaries, creating a sophisticated and internationally-minded approach to visual kei. Their musical identity blended the theatrical, androgynous visual presentation characteristic of the genre with a softer, more elegant sonic palette, appealing to audiences who sought visual kei that transcended purely Japanese cultural references.

The group gained recognition through compelling tracks such as "Je n'veux pas rester sage" and "鬱雪ドロップ" (Utsu Yuki Drop), showcasing their ability to balance melancholic atmospherics with rock sensibilities. Their work demonstrated how visual kei could incorporate European influences and female vocal leadership while maintaining the genre's core theatrical and emotional intensity. Through songs like "Partir seule," Dolly contributed to the diversification of visual kei in the 2000s era, proving that the genre's boundaries were expansive enough to accommodate artists who drew inspiration from global sources while honoring the aesthetic and philosophical foundations of the visual kei movement.
